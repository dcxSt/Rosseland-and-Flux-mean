The Final Word on Computing Rosseland and Flux Mean Opacities*

*in Hot, Isotropic, Degenerate, Optically Thick, Locally Thermalized, Scattering Dominated Media

Paper: https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.3847/1538-4357/835/2/119

Juri Poutanen, author of the paper we are reviewing, is a high-energy astrophysicist in Finland. He studies stellar interiors and X-ray bursts.


Introduction

Radiative transport is one of two pillars of astrophysical heat transport that underpin our knowledge of many (all?) astrophysical processes. The Rosseland and Flux mean opacities provide a way to compress a whole spectrum's worth of energy-transfer information into a single number, as such they are essential and ubiquitous tools in the arsenal of astrophysicists.

In his paper, Juri Poutanen derives an exact expression for the Rosseland and Flux mean opacities from basic physics; this being the case, we have no choice but to get down and dirty with some equations. However, we will not be as assiduous in our book-keeping as Poutanen is in the paper, opting instead to select a few key equations and pick them appart for physical intuition. (If you want to see the derivations in all their glory you can read the paper. If that doesn't quench your appetite there's an 83 page monograph on the minutae of Compton Scattering penned by the same hand.)

Rosseland Mean Opacity

\[ \begin{equation}\label{eq:RMO} {1 \over \kappa_R} = {\int_0^\infty d\nu \kappa_\nu^{-1}(\partial B_\nu / \partial T) \over \int_0^\infty d\nu (\partial B_\nu / \partial T)} \end{equation} \]

Radiation transfer is what astrophysicists call “how light propagates in different media”. Media are “backgrounds” through which light travels: e.g. a gas of ionized hydrogen at 3000 Kelvin, is one example of a medium astrophysicists may be interested in—or, less abstractly, the inside of the sun is another.

One of the important parameters in determining the dynamics of radiation transfer through a medium is its opacity. The opacity quantifies the rate of scattering (photons bouncing off of things) and absorption (photons being gobbled up by things). The opacity of a medium is frequency dependent, i.e. media typically absorb and scatter different frequencies of light differently.

The Rosseland mean opacity is a weighted frequency-average of the opacity of a medium. Weight is ascribed proportionally to $\partial B_\nu / \partial T$—the temperature derivative of the black-body, because this quantity measures how efficient that frequency is at transporting energy down thermal gradients. The Rosseland mean opacity is a useful tool in the hands of astronomers because it enables a simplified treatment of radiative transfer.

Flux Mean Opacity

\[ \begin{equation}\label{eq:FMO} \kappa_F = \int_0^\infty d\nu \kappa_\nu F_\nu / F \end{equation} \]
The flux mean opacity relates the bolometric radiation flux to the radiative acceleration.
Juri Poutanen, Rosseland and Flux Mean Opacities for Compton Scattering

Here, bolometric refers to measurements or quantities that account for radiation over all wavelengths, not just a limited portion of the electromagnetic spectrum. The bolometric radiation flux represents the total energy emitted per unit area across all wavelengths.

Radiative acceleration refers to the acceleration imparted to matter by radiation pressure. Light exerts pressure when it interacts with matter, this pressure can cause matter to accelerate. Radiative acceleration drives stellar winds, it influences the dynamics of accretion disks around compact objects, and it affects the structure and evolution of stars.

Compton Scattering and the RTE

Equation \eqref{eq:RTE0} shows the Radiation Transfer Equation (RTE) that we know and love.

\[ \begin{equation}\label{eq:RTE0} {d I_\nu \over d s} = -\sigma_nu I_\nu + \int_{4\pi} \sigma_nu(\Omega’ \to \Omega)I_\nu(\Omega’)d\Omega’ \end{equation} \]

Compton scattering is a photon-electron inelastic collision. It's "inelastic" because energy is echanged between photons and the electrons, this only happens when the electrons are hot. Compton scattering is an innately quantum phenomenon, so classical EM waves won’t help us here.

Compton Scattering Analogy: A photon—a quanta of the EM field—has some probability of scattering off of an electron. We like to think about photons as being located in some particular place rather than in a superposition of many places at once, and, thankfully, this “billiard ball” imagery is applicable if we are willing to take a few QED-derived (Quantum Electro Dynamics) formulae on good faith.

Starting from the Relativistic Kinetic Equation for Compton scattering (a dumb equation which describes the macroscopic, statistical behaviour of populations of photons and electrons in terms of their microscopic properties), Poutanen guids us to the RTE. We will gloss over the derivation but pick apart the resulting RTE as it is the wellspring from which all knowledge flows.

\[ \begin{align*} \hat \omega \cdot \nabla_\tau n(\mathbf x) = &-n(\mathbf{x}){1\over x} \int_0^\infty x_1dx_1 \int d^2\hat\omega_1 R(x_1,x,\mu)\\ &+{1\over x} \int_0^\infty x_1dx_1 \int d^2\hat\omega_1 R(x,x_1,\mu)\\ &-n(\mathbf x){1 \over x} \int_0^\infty x_1dx_1 \int d^2\hat\omega_1 n(\mathbf x_1) [R(x_1,x,\mu) - R(x,x_1,\mu)] \end{align*} \]

The above expression may look alien but rest assured, it is merely a familiar face couched in the trappings of foreign notation (it's Equation \eqref{eq:RTE0}). First we see that there are only four terms, one on the left hand side (LHS) and three on the right hand side (RHS).

LHS: \(\hat \omega\) is the direction of propagation, \(\mathbf x\) is the four momentum of the incident photons and it therefore encodes frequency as well as direction, \(n(\mathbf x)\) is the photon number density. Since \(I_x \propto n_x x\), we see that the left hand side is related to \({d I_\nu \over d s}\) by a factor of the frequency \(x \equiv \nu\)—the author chose to put it’s reciprocal on the left-hand side instead.

RHS: \(\mathbf x_1\) is the four-momentum of the scattered photon, \(\mu\) is the cosine of the scattering angle, and \(R(x,x_1,\mu)\) is the redistribution function which quantifies the rate at which a photon with energy $x$ will scatter into a photon with energy $x_1$, at scattering angle is \(\mu\). As intimidating as they look, there are only three terms, and, two of them are familiar. The first is negative, and it’s proportional to \(n(\mathbf x)\), just like \(-\sigma_\nu I_\nu\). The second is positive, and looks alot like the in-scattering term \(\int \sigma_\nu (\Omega’ \to \Omega )d\Omega’\). The final term is a bit awkward and it may have to do with photon-photon interactions (don't ask me); suffice it to say, that if \(n(\mathbf x)<<1\), the term is negligible and when we are in regimes where the redistribution is roughly symmetric in it’s first two arguments \(R(x,x_1,\mu)\approx R(x_1,x,\mu)\), it is also negligible.

Now all we have to do is figure out what the redistribution function is and then we know the full RTE (in other words EVERYTHING).

The Redistribution Function

The all important redistribution function (RF) encapsulates the microscopic physics of photon-electron interactions. The electron distribution also factors into the integrand of RF (which can be expressed as an integral, see equaiton 11 in the paper).

For the electrons, we take the Fermi-Dirac distribution (isotropic), since we are interested in the degenerate regime. Using this isotropic distribution implies that we are in an isotropic and locally thermalized (LTE) medium.

Another ingredient that enters the dynamics of these interactions is the Klein-Nishina reaction rate, which is related to the Klein-Nishina cross section. The reaction rate (and the cross section) is dependent on the four-momenta of the incoming and scattered photon. This aditional element of complication is required to deal with relativistic electrons. (And the electrons that inhabit the nuclear furnaces of X-ray-bursting neutron stars are very much relativistic.)

Cross Section Analogy: Imagine the electrons are billiard balls and photons are like ping-pong balls, and they're all bouncing around the place. The cross-section of a particle is kindof like the $\pi r^2$, where $r$ is the ball's radius. The greater the cross section, the likelier two passing balls will hit each other. Unfortunately, the Klein-Nishina cross section is dependent on speed and direction of incident. This means that the billiard balls' sizes appear larger to ping-pong balls (photons) that it is speeding towards that ping pong balls that it's travelling perpendicularly to. This is quite unintuitive to picture. That's special relativity.

To get at a manageable expression for the distribution of photons, we expand around the black-body distribution to first order. This is justified because in the thermonuclear burning regions we are concerned with, the radiation field is nearly isotropic. The non-black-body term depends on the (frequency dependent) mean-free path $l_x$.

Remember that to find the Rosseland and Flux mean opacities—our ultimate goal—we need to integrate over the frequency dependent opacity as well as the frequency dependent flux; both of which we can compute once we know $n(\mathbf x)$ and $l_x$. Thankfully, a linear integral equation for the mean-free path—which can be solved numerically, by iteration, as we did on the first problem set—pops right out of the RTE after we substitute in the photon distribution black-body expanded expression.

Results

Here's the punchline: Equation \eqref{eq:punchline}

\[ \begin{equation}\label{eq:punchline} \kappa_R \propto {\int_0^\infty {\partial B_x \over \partial \Theta} dx \over \int_0^\infty l_x {\partial B_x \over \partial \Theta} dx} \end{equation} \]

We're done! A similar expression can be found for the Flux mean opacity. Sure, we’ve made a few approximations, but all of them hold water in the regimes of interest to Juri Poutanen. Notably, electron degeneracy and relativistic effects have been accounted for.

We are now poised to reap the rewards of Juri Poutanen's labour by using this result by reviewing the litterature on previousy known approximations. Luckily, Poutanen did that for us too!

Conclusion / My Take

What struck me about this paper was the analytical sophistication of Poutanen’s result. It appears to be the final word on the computation of both the Rosseland mean opacity and the flux mean opacity in these high-energy regimes. I'm impressed by Poutanen's ability to make his findings legible to a wide audience and to package them into a coherent 8-page bombshell of a paper.

The qualification I put in the title is obviously a bit of a joke at the expense of Poutanen, but it's not a very cutting one because the result is much more general and generalizable than the joke would have you believe. Half of those qualifiers are sound assumptions in many astrophysical contexts (LTE, Optically Thick, Hot), and the result also holds in the non-degenerate case. Furthermore, something I glossed over in this review was that absorbption was accounted for! Finally, the assumption that the scattering agents are isotropically distributed is good because (a) it simplifies the reader's job, and (b) this paper lays the ground-work for computing non-isotropic distributions too: just figure out how to incorporate that into the redistribution function and you're done!

This paper was published in Feburary 2017 and has accrued 40 citations as of Feburary 2024. I sampled 15 of those citations and they were split about 50/50 between papers on stellar evolution and papers on neutron stars. Nevertheless, it's hard to evaluate the importance of this paper without being familiar with the field. (I didn't trouble myself to read those papers to find out how critical Poutanen's work was to them.)

It definitely seems like a useful piece of work; Poutanen found errors in one of the approximations used in the litterature and he was able to show us explicitly in what temperature and degeneracy regimes other approximations become invalid. I doubt it voids any past work but it does empower us to discern the accuracy of past results that made use of dubious approximations. Moreover, it paves the way for more precise simulations of X-ray bursts and solar interiors and it gives us more confidance in future simulation results. Perhaps a future paper will do the same derivation but with an anisotropic electron distribution to model strong magnetic fields.

Unfortunately I couldn't find Juri Poutanen on GitHub and was unable to scrutinize his code; though I have messaged him about this. I also felt that there was a computational optimization section missing from this paper; we were never shown how fast the $l_x$ iterations converged.

TL;DR

This paper is important for reasons:

  • Sets previously unknown systematic errors on historic results.
  • Enables calculation of energy-transport in previously unknown regions of parameter space.
  • Methods lay groundwork for calculations involving more complicated scattering-agent distribution functions.
